Arroz Con Gandules

Few dishes summon nostalgic memories of family and festivity quite like arroz con gandules, a fragrant and beloved Puerto Rican classic. You can usually find it at family reunions, Christmas dinner, New Year’s celebrations or any other festive holiday. Normally, it is served alongside pernil — the king of the holiday table and the chief representative of togetherness and heritage. It is accompanied by ensalada de coditos — a slightly sweet and tangy creamy macaroni salad. Translated, the name means rice with pigeon peas. However, this seemingly straightforward meal is surprisingly complex in how its flavors are layered and its colors orchestrated. Its intoxicating aroma acts as a siren as it calls the attention of the uninitiated and has the ability to turn even the pickiest of eaters into fans.

Ingredients

  • Bacon or Ham
  • Adobo
  • Sazon Goya
  • Onion
  • Garlic
  • culantro
  • Aji dulce
  • Olive oil
  • Pigeon Peas
  • Olives
  • Rice

Recipes and approaches to this dish are as numerous as the population on the island, so in order to keep things simple, I will be sharing my mother’s recipe, which captures her idiosyncrasies while also staying faithful to traditional conceptions.

She would start off her rendition by cubing either bacon or ham, this decision was usually made for her as it was determined by whatever she had available. Once cubed, she would throw the pieces into a large, cold, round bottom aluminum pot known as a caldero. Unbeknownst to many, the caldero is actually one of the ingredients for this recipe as it plays an integral role in the formation of pegao, or the crispy rice that is left behind in the pot that serves as a delicious savory, nutty snack for those that love different textures.

Once settled in the pot, she would set her gas range stove to medium heat, lighting the gas with a lighter since the pilot did not work and the only thing one would hear is click-click-click-click. Now with the flame burning, the fat would begin to render and the house would be inundated with the smell of breakfast. Once most of the fat was rendered, the meat would turn golden brown and the bottom of the pan would be plated with fond. At this stage, my mother would then add her homemade sofrito, since the frozen version from the store just wasn’t up to her standards. To most accurately describe what sofrito is and why it is the cornerstone of all Puerto Rican cooking, it’s best to first list what makes up this concoction.

A blend of onion, garlic, cilantro, culantro, sweet peppers, and a splash of olive oil; sofrito is the foundation of countless Hispanic Caribbean recipes and the flavor base of our Arroz con gandules. Once added to the pot, this herbaceous mixture releases an aroma so intoxicating, the inhabitants of the house where this dish is being prepared will respond like:

Once aromatic, the next steps happen quickly. Pigeon peas, olives, adobe seasoning, a packet of sazón and the rice are all added to the pot. Quickly, these ingredients are mixed and allowed to get to know one another. Once properly introduced and married, just enough water and beer (my mother’s secret) was then added to cover the rice and the pot was then brought to a boil. She always said that the alcohol would boil off but it would impart another layer of depth. Once boiling, the flame was lowered to lo, and the caldero was topped with its lid. It would only be about twenty more minutes until the lid could be removed, the rice fluffed and the festivities could begin properly.
